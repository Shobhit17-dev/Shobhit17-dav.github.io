The diversity of species

1. Diversity in the ecosystem

Some of the most iconic species, such as the black-maned African lions, wildebeest, African elephants, two horned rhinos among several can be found in the Serengeti ecosystem. The population differs from species to species, depending on several factors such as body size, movement patterns, reproduction cycles, and direct (as well as indirect) interactions with other species in the ecosystem. As can be seen in the visualization, the wildebeest population surpasses all the other species by orders of magnitude.

Communities exist within the ecosystem, where the species are directly or indirectly interdependent on each other.

2. Community associated with the wildebeest

A unique characteristic of the wildebeest are their incredibly large numbers. Nowehere else in the entire world can such a large population of ungulates be found.

The wildebeest, zebra and Thomson's gazelle constitute the migratory species within this community. They share common paths during migration, for most of the year. They are even interdependent on each other in terms of feeding preferences.

This large group of migrating ungulates (wildebeest in particular) have many far-reaching effects on the entire community, otherwise known as a keystone effect. As discussed earlier, such an effect leads to increased biodiversity.

The configuration of the overall community has been shown in the visualization.

Short grass

It is the most preferred diet for the wildebeest in particular, among various other small-to-medium sized herbivores. Short grass grows abundantly in the southern plains of the Serengeti. However, it is only available in this area during the wet season.

Lion

The famous black-maned lions of the Serengeti are the apex predators of the ecosystem. Their most preferred prey is the wildebeest. However, they also hunt zebra, gazelle and buffalo.

Buffalo

The buffalo is a large herbivore that also prefers eating short grass. It is residential in nature, and hence is regulated from the top-down. However, its sheer strength makes it a formidable opponent for its predators.

Zebra

Although their path of migration slightly differs from that of the wildebeest, they graze the southern plains alongside them. This strategy helps them avoid being spotted by predators. Being a migrant, they are also able to counter both types of regulation.

Wildebeest

As the diagram depicts, they are the keystone species of this community. Their migration directly influences the availability of short grass across the Serengeti, leading to several indirect effects. Their overall impact is disproportionately large compared to their already immense population.

Thomson's Gazelle

A herbivore that grazes in the company of wildebeest and zebra. It is much smaller in size compared to its fellow migrants, making it an easier target for predators. However, migration allows it to counter both types of regulation, resulting in the third largest population in the ecosystem.

Giraffe

This iconic creature prefers to feed on fresh leaves that grow on the high branches of trees. Being a mega-herbivore, it is not a preferred prey for the predators of the Serengeti. Hence, it is regulated entirely from the bottom-up. Its population is indirectly affected by the presence of wildebeest in the ecosystem.

Elephant

This imposing mega-herbivore experiences almost zero predation. Predators can only prey on the calfs sometimes, if the herd is unable to shield them. The elephant browses through entire trees (especially yearlings), hence relying on their availability for survival. Their population indirectly is affected by the presence of wildebeest as well.

Hyena

Hyenas are as lethal a predator as lions, if not more. They track their prey for several kilometers in the hopes of wearing them out. In the Serengeti, they prey on the buffalo, wildebeest, zebra and Thomson's gazelle, among other species.

Tree

The trees (especially the tree yearlings) make up a crucial component of the ecosystem. They provide nourishment for the mega-herbivores such as elephants and giraffes. Tree yearlings are destroyed due to fires, which require dry short grass as fuel. Hence the wildebeest are indirectly responsible for the increased growth in tree numbers.
