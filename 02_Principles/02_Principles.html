Principles governing life in the Serengeti

1. Regulation

To understand regulation, it is imperative to be aware of the fact that any physical environment is only capable of producing a limited amount of food/resources.

The principle of regulation states that the population of every species is ‘regulated’ by some other biotic component of the ecosystem. For example, the oribi population is kept in check by the lions. However, the elephant population is maintained by the limited availability of tree leaves.

If the population of a species is maintained by their predators, they're said to be 'top-down' regulated. However, if it is maintained due to the limited availability of food in the environment, they're said to be 'bottom-up' regulated.

The direction of regulation is mainly dependent on the movement pattern and size of individuals within a species. It is also decided by the diet, competition, and accessibility to food (dependent on physical environment and climate).

2. Migration

Migration is a movement pattern that results in a seasonal round trip. Animals who follow this pattern are known as migrants. Some of the migrant species found in the Serengeti include zebra, Thomson’s gazelle, wildebeest, eland, and elephants.

Animals who do not migrate are known as residents. Many predators, meso-predators, and herbivores such as buffalo, giraffe, rhino, topi, kongoni, oribi, and impala constitute the resident species.

Migration directly affects the fundamental principle of regulation. For example, migration lends wildebeest the advantage of counteracting both Bottom-Up and Top-Down regulation. Hence, migrant species tend to have (depending on their reproductive cycles as well) larger populations compared to residents.

It is important to note that migration is a direct consequence of climate.

3. Keystone effects

Drawing from the analogy of a masonry arch, this effect is created by the presence of a single species in the ecosystem or even a community. It can be identified by the direct and indirect effects the species has on several others. Like a keystone in an arch, its removal would result in the collapse of the overall configuration, hence lending it the status of a 'keystone species'.

The total number of interdependent species present in an ecosystem or community is termed as its 'biodiversity'. The indirect effects caused on the rest of the community by a keystone species lead to an increase in the biodiversity.

The constancy in the population size or the number of species is termed as the 'stability' of an ecosystem. The ability of an ecosystem to buffer disturbances from the environment is known as its 'resilience'. It has been proven that an increase in the biodiversity of an ecosystem results in increased stability and resilience as well.
