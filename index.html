The Serengeti National Park is considered to encompass one of the last remaining pristine natural ecosystems on Earth. Booming with life, this place has been of interest to scientists from across the globe. Many teams of scientists have been conducting research to elucidate the underlying mechanisms and aspects of the ecosystem, many of which have been quite elusive. Over a period of 50 years, their work has led to interesting discoveries that explain how ecosystems work.

This interactive prototype aims to explain the basic rules that govern the Serengeti ecosystem, and also ecosystems around the world.

Contextual Information

The Serengeti ecosystem was at the top of the vote list for World Heritage sites at a U.N. convention concerning the Protection of the World Cultural and Natural Heritage, in 1972. Its uniqueness lies in its persistence for over 4 million years, environmental factors that allow for large-scale migrations and an amazing biodiversity.

Location & constitution

The Serengeti National Park (SNP) is located in Tanzania, Africa. It contains the Ngorongoro Conservation Area (NCA) towards the east. It also contains a large part of the Serengeti-Mara Ecosystem, which has a total area of 25,000 km2. The extent of this ecosystem is defined by the movement of the migratory wildebeest. The Maasai Mara Reserve lies to the north of the SNP, within the confines of Kenya.

Microclimate & habitats

The neighbouring lake Victoria is gigantic enough to cause a three-zoned climatic gradient to exist over the ecosystem. It ranges from a wet north-western region to a dry south-eastern region.

In the Serengeti, habitats starkly vary along this climatic gradient. Each of these makes for a unique set of conditions and indicates the preference a species has over the physical environment.

The Serengeti is known for its vast grasslands, found in the south and south-eastern parts of the ecosystem. This is where the great migration can be seen to unfold, while a variety of fauna congregates during the wet season. However, the availability of grass in this area isnt everlasting.

Trophic Levels

Several species of fauna coexist in the Serengeti ecosystem. Some of the most iconic ones might be the African black-maned lions, wildebeest, African elephants, and the two horned rhinoceros. Each species has its own preferred habitat and niche.

They all exist on different trophic levels. A species on a higher trophic level consumes those on lower levels. This interaction between species is known as a trophic interaction. The visualization to the left is a representation of this.

Rules that govern ecosystems

Any ecosystem can only sustain a given number of individuals. The reason for this is that it can only produce a limited amount of resources. To better understand this, think of it as energy available in the environment for consumption. It is eventually passed on from one species to another, though the food chain. The limited nature of this energy is key to understanding the rules mentioned hereafter.

Movement patterns in animals

Migration is a movement pattern that results in a seasonal round trip. Animals who follow this pattern are known as migrants. Some of the migrant species found in the Serengeti include zebra, Thomsons gazelle, wildebeest, eland, and elephants.

Animals who do not migrate are known as residents. Many predators, meso-predators, and herbivores such as buffalo, giraffe, rhino, topi, kongoni, oribi, and impala constitute the resident species.

The advantage of Migration

Migration directly affects the fundamental principle of regulation.

For example, migration lends the wildebeest an advantage of counteracting both Bottom-Up and Top-Down regulation. Hence, migrant species tend to have (depending on their reproductive cycles) larger populations compared to residents.

It is important to note that migration is an indirect consequence of changes in the season.

The Principle of Regulation

The principle of regulation states that the population of every species is regulated by some other biotic component of the ecosystem. For example, the oribi population is kept in check by the lions. However, the elephant population is maintained by the limited availability of tree leaves.

If the population of a species is maintained by their predators, they're said to be 'top-down' regulated. However, if it is maintained due to the limited availability of food in the environment, they're said to be 'bottom-up' regulated.

The direction of regulation is majorly dependent on the movement pattern, size of individuals, and the inherent nature of a species.

Keystone effects

Drawing from the analogy of a masonry arch, this effect is created by the presence of a single species in the ecosystem or a community. It can be recognized by the direct and indirect effects the species has on several others. Like a keystone in an arch, its removal would result in the collapse of the overall configuration, hence lending it the status of a 'keystone species'.

The total number of interdependent species present in an ecosystem or community is termed as its 'biodiversity'. The indirect effects caused on the rest of the community by a keystone species lead to an increase in the biodiversity.

The constancy in the population size or the number of species is termed as the 'stability' of an ecosystem. The ability of an ecosystem to buffer disturbances from the environment is known as its 'resilience'. It has been proven that an increase in the biodiversity of an ecosystem results in increased stability and resilience as well.

An interesting community of animals

Within an ecosystem, smaller communities can exist where the species are directly or indirectly interdependent on each other. This forms a sort of tightly knit sub-system within the larger ecosystem. Once such community has been of interest to researchers, comprising several ungulates.

During the early 1960s, scientists stationed in Serengeti N.P. observed certain anomalies in the ungulate population. Some of the species (the wildebeest in particular) were reaching alarmingly high numbers. This was a cause of concern since they could potentially destroy the ecosystem by overgrazing. However, the scientists decided to let nature take its course and observe the changes. To their surprise, the ecosystem started to flourish even more after a decade.
To understand this phenomenon, we must look backwards to when the ungulate population was perceivably normal. Among the ungulates, the wildebeest, zebra, Thomsons gazelle and buffalo were most affected by Rinderpest. Hence, our focus shall be specifically on these species.

The Rinderpest Outbreak

Rinderpest was a viral disease that was brought into Africa by invaders coming from India. It originally infected the cattle population and eventually caused a 95 percent wipeout. This in turn caused famines and lots of people died as a result. The cattle even infected the wildlife, causing their population to come down as well. This phenomenon was, however, unknown at the time.

Eventually a widescale vaccination program was launched that eradicated the virus between 1963-64. This allowed for the ungulate population to bounce back.

Conclusion

In summary, it is necessary to first understand the basic principle of regulation and other concepts such as migration, keystone effects, biodiversity, stability, and resilience in order to prevent ecosystems from switching into irreparable states and configurations. Thoughtless human activity like the destruction of rainforests for farming and infrastructure development are testing the resilience of age-old ecosystems and might even force them to collapse.

As the keystone species of the world, we must start being more sensitive towards the inner mechanisms of the natural habitats around us. The application of these simple ecological principles has even revived the biodiversity of crumbling ecosystems, such as in the case of Kruger National Park. Such accomplishments are proof of the amazing regenerative capabilities that nature possesses.
